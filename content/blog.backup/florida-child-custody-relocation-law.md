Moving to Tampa Bay with kids when you're sharing custody? Florida's relocation laws are about as straightforward as Interstate 4 traffic during rush hour — meaning they're not. After helping 800+ families navigate moves across the Tampa Bay area over the past 23 years, I've seen firsthand how custody complications can derail an otherwise smooth relocation.

Here's what every parent needs to know about Florida's child custody and relocation laws before making that move to Clearwater, St. Pete, or anywhere else in our beautiful corner of the state.

## The 50-Mile Rule: Florida's Moving Boundary

Florida Statute 61.13001 draws a bright line at 50 miles. Move within that radius? You're generally good to go with proper notice. Move beyond it? You're entering court territory.

But here's where it gets tricky — those 50 miles are measured from the child's current primary residence, not from some arbitrary point. So if your ex lives in Brandon and you want to move to Clearwater Beach, that's roughly 35 miles. But if they're in Plant City and you're eyeing Dunedin, you're pushing 55 miles and crossing into petition territory.

The law distinguishes between two types of moves:

**Temporary relocation** (60 days or less): Requires written notice to the other parent and the court, but typically doesn't need approval unless the other parent objects.

**Permanent relocation** (more than 60 days): This is where things get complicated fast. You'll need either written consent from the other parent or court approval.

### What Counts as "50 Miles"?

The statute specifies this distance "from his or her principal place of residence." Courts typically measure this as straight-line distance, not driving miles. That matters when you're looking at moves across Tampa Bay where bridges add significant travel time even if the crow-flies distance seems reasonable.

I've seen parents get surprised when a move from South Tampa to Safety Harbor — which feels close culturally — still triggers the 50-mile rule because of the actual geographic distance.

## When You Need Court Approval

If you're moving more than 50 miles permanently, or if the other parent objects to any relocation, you're filing a petition. Florida courts don't rubber-stamp these requests. They're looking at what's truly in the child's best interest, not just your career opportunity or housing preferences.

### Required Elements for Your Petition

Your petition must include specific information:

- The new address (or as specific as available)
- The moving date
- Detailed reasons for the relocation
- Proposed revised time-sharing schedule
- Transportation arrangements for the child
- How you'll maintain the child's relationship with the other parent

### The Best Interest Analysis

Florida Statute 61.13001 lists 11 specific factors courts must consider. The big ones I see deciding cases in Tampa Bay:

**The child's relationship with both parents.** Courts heavily weigh how the move affects the non-relocating parent's ability to maintain a meaningful relationship. A move to Orlando might look different than a move to Seattle.

**The child's age and developmental needs.** Younger children generally have more flexibility. High schoolers? That's a much harder sell, especially if they're established in Brandon High School or Countryside High School with friends and activities.

**The economic necessity and benefit of relocation.** "I got a better job" isn't automatically compelling. Courts want to see that this move significantly improves the child's life, not just yours.

**Educational opportunities.** Moving from Pinellas County Schools (rated B+ overall) to a district with significantly better ratings can strengthen your case. Moving to a worse district? That's a problem.

**Geographic considerations and logistical factors.** This includes travel time, cost, and the child's age-appropriate involvement in decision-making.

## The Notice Requirements

Even for moves within 50 miles, Florida law requires specific notice procedures. This isn't a casual "hey, we're moving" text message.

### Written Notice Must Include:

- Your intended new address
- The date of intended relocation  
- A statement of specific reasons for the proposed relocation
- A proposal for a revised time-sharing schedule
- A proposal regarding transportation of the child
- A statement that the other parent may file an objection

### Timing Matters

Notice must be provided **at least 60 days before relocation**. Not 59 days. Not "as soon as possible." Exactly 60 days minimum. I've seen cases where parents lost significant credibility with judges over inadequate notice timing.

If you don't know your exact address yet (common when relocating for work), provide as much detail as possible and supplement when you have specifics. The key is demonstrating good faith effort to keep the other parent informed.

## Common Tampa Bay Relocation Scenarios

### Within the Bay Area Moves

Most relocations I help with stay within our region, and many avoid the 50-mile trigger:

- **Tampa to St. Petersburg**: About 25 miles — generally no court approval needed
- **Clearwater to Brandon**: Roughly 40 miles — still within the safe zone
- **Pinellas Park to Wesley Chapel**: Approximately 45 miles — cutting it close but usually okay

### Cross-State Line Moves

Moving to Lakeland (45 miles from downtown Tampa) keeps you under 50 miles. But crossing into Georgia, even to Jacksonville (140 miles), definitely requires court approval.

### Job-Related Relocations

I see this frequently with MacDill Air Force Base families, Tampa General Hospital specialists, and corporate relocations. Courts are more sympathetic when the move is genuinely necessary for employment, but you still need to demonstrate how it benefits the child.

## What Happens If the Other Parent Objects

When the other parent files an objection, you're headed to a hearing. In Hillsborough County, these cases typically get scheduled within 30-45 days, though complex cases can take longer.

### Preparing for Your Hearing

**Documentation is everything.** Bring job offers, school information, housing details, and a comprehensive parenting plan showing how you'll maintain the child's relationship with their other parent.

**Transportation plans matter.** If you're moving to Jacksonville and expecting the other parent to drive to Orlando for exchanges, that's not going to fly. Courts expect reasonable transportation arrangements, often splitting driving duties or meeting halfway.

**Maintain status quo until decided.** Don't move before the hearing unless there's an emergency. Judges take a dim view of parents who relocate first and ask permission later.

## Protecting Your Case

### Start Early

Begin documenting your reasons for relocation months before you need to move. If it's job-related, keep all correspondence with employers. If it's for family support, document those circumstances.

### School Research

If you're moving for better educational opportunities, research thoroughly. Print school report cards, test scores, and program offerings. Pinellas County has excellent schools, but if you're moving to a district with even better ratings, document that advantage.

### Maintain Relationships

The court wants to see that you value the child's relationship with the other parent. Demonstrate flexibility in scheduling, willingness to handle transportation, and creative solutions for maintaining contact.

## Working Around the Rules

### Gradual Moves

Some parents successfully relocate by making incremental moves that each fall within 50 miles, eventually reaching their desired destination. This is legally permissible but can be practically complicated and expensive.

### Shared Custody Adjustments

Sometimes the solution isn't fighting the relocation but restructuring the parenting arrangement. If the other parent agrees to adjusted time-sharing that accommodates the distance, you might avoid court entirely.

### Temporary Orders

If you need to move quickly for employment, courts can issue temporary relocation orders while the permanent petition is pending. These require showing immediate necessity and typically include conditions for maintaining the child's relationship with the non-relocating parent.

## The Cost Factor

Relocation cases aren't cheap. Attorney fees typically run $5,000-15,000+ depending on complexity. Court costs, expert witnesses, and evaluation fees add to the expense. Factor these costs into your moving budget alongside your standard [moving expenses](/cost-of-living/).

Guardian ad litem appointments (required in many contested cases) add another $2,000-5,000. These court-appointed advocates investigate and make recommendations about what's best for the child.

## Alternative Solutions

### Mediation First

Many judges require mediation before scheduling hearings. This can be significantly less expensive than litigation and gives you more control over the outcome. Tampa Bay has excellent family mediators who understand local dynamics.

### Collaborative Law

This newer approach involves both parents working with specially trained attorneys to reach agreements outside court. It's particularly effective for parents who want to maintain a positive co-parenting relationship post-relocation.

## Practical Tampa Bay Considerations

### School District Transitions

Moving between Hillsborough and Pinellas County schools requires advance planning. Both districts have good reputations, but specific programs vary significantly. Countryside High School's IB program isn't available everywhere, and Plant High School's football tradition matters to some families.

### Transportation and Logistics

Tampa Bay's geography creates unique challenges. What looks like 40 miles on a map might involve bridge traffic that makes regular exchanges impractical. Factor in actual travel times during peak hours, not just GPS estimates.

Weekend exchanges that used to take 20 minutes might now require 90 minutes each way. Courts consider these practical realities when evaluating relocation requests.

### Community Connections

Document your child's current activities and research equivalent options in your new location. Youth sports leagues, music programs, and social connections matter to judges evaluating best interests.

## Post-Relocation Compliance

### Modified Parenting Plans

Once you receive approval to relocate, ensure your parenting plan reflects the new reality. This includes holiday schedules, summer vacation arrangements, and transportation responsibilities.

### Ongoing Communication

Maintaining regular communication becomes more critical after relocation. Courts often require specific technology provisions for video calls, shared photo apps, and other tools to preserve relationships.

### Annual Reviews

Some relocation orders include annual review provisions, allowing the non-relocating parent to petition for modifications if circumstances change. Understanding these provisions helps avoid future surprises.

## When Plans Change

Life happens. Job opportunities fall through, housing deals collapse, or family circumstances change. If you've received court approval to relocate but can't or won't move, notify the court and the other parent immediately.

Similarly, if the other parent relocates after initially objecting to your move, you might have grounds to reopen your petition. Florida law recognizes that custody arrangements should evolve with changing circumstances.

## Getting Professional Help

Family law attorneys specializing in relocation cases understand Florida's specific requirements and local court preferences. This isn't the time for general practice attorneys or DIY legal forms.

Look for attorneys with specific experience in your county. Hillsborough County judges have different temperaments and preferences than Pinellas County judges. Local knowledge matters.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Can I move within Florida without court approval if it's under 50 miles?

Yes, but you still must provide proper written notice at least 60 days before relocating. The other parent can still object and force a court hearing even for moves under 50 miles, though courts are generally more permissive for shorter distances.

### What happens if I move without permission or proper notice?

Moving without following Florida's relocation laws can result in contempt of court charges, immediate orders to return the child, modification of custody arrangements in the other parent's favor, and significant attorney fees. Courts take unauthorized relocations very seriously.

### How long does the court approval process typically take?

In Tampa Bay area courts, expect 60-90 days minimum from filing your petition to getting a decision. Complex cases with extensive evidence or expert testimony can take 4-6 months or longer.

### Can the other parent stop me from moving even if I don't take the children?

No, Florida law doesn't restrict your personal relocation. However, if you move far enough away that it affects your ability to exercise your current time-sharing schedule, the other parent could petition for a custody modification based on changed circumstances.

### What if my ex-spouse agrees to the move verbally but won't sign papers?

Verbal agreements aren't sufficient for legal relocation in Florida. You need written consent or court approval. If they agree verbally, push for written documentation or file your petition to get court approval as backup protection.

### Are temporary moves for military deployment handled differently?

Yes, Florida law has special provisions for military families. Temporary duty assignments typically don't require the full relocation process, and courts can't use temporary military relocations against service members in permanent custody decisions.

### Can I relocate if I share 50/50 custody with the other parent?

Shared custody doesn't eliminate relocation requirements, but it does make court approval more challenging. You'll need to demonstrate compelling reasons why relocation serves the child's best interests despite disrupting the current equal arrangement.

### What if my child wants to move with me but the other parent objects?

Children's preferences matter more as they get older, but it's just one factor among many. A 16-year-old's wishes carry more weight than a 10-year-old's, but judges focus primarily on objective best interest factors rather than children's stated preferences alone.
