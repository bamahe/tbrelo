## Florida Has No State Income Tax — Here's What That Actually Means

The number one financial reason people move to Florida is simple: no state income tax. It's not a gimmick, not a limited-time thing, and not just for retirees. It's written into the Florida Constitution (Article VII, Section 5), which means it would take a voter-approved constitutional amendment to change it. That's about as permanent as tax law gets.

For someone earning $100K who moves from New York (where the state + city tax rate can hit 12%+), that's roughly $8,000–$12,000 back in your pocket every year. From California? Even more. From New Jersey or Connecticut? Similar story. That's not a tax deduction — that's cash you keep.

But "no income tax" doesn't mean "no taxes." Florida still collects revenue, and understanding where the money comes from will prevent some surprises. This guide covers every tax you'll encounter as a Florida resident, with real numbers for Tampa Bay's eight counties.

## The Tax You Won't Pay — State Income Tax

Florida is one of nine states with no personal income tax. Here's what that covers:

- **Wages and salary** — zero state tax
- **Self-employment income** — zero state tax
- **Retirement income** (pensions, 401k withdrawals, IRA distributions) — zero state tax
- **Social Security benefits** — zero state tax (also exempt federally for most)
- **Capital gains** — zero state tax
- **Rental income** — zero state tax
- **Investment dividends and interest** — zero state tax

You still pay federal income tax on all of the above. Florida can't help you there. But the state portion? Gone.

### What's Taxed and What's Not

**Taxed:** Most retail purchases, electronics, furniture, clothing, restaurant meals, prepared food, alcohol, cars, boats, services like pest control and security monitoring.

**Not taxed:** Groceries (unprepared food), prescription medications, some medical equipment, residential rent (though commercial rent is taxed at 2% state + surtax).

The grocery exemption is important — your weekly Publix run for unprepared food is tax-free. But the rotisserie chicken from the deli? Taxed, because it's prepared food.

### Sales Tax on Big Purchases

If you're buying a car, boat, or other major purchase, Florida caps the sales tax on the first $5,000 of the purchase price at the state rate, then the full rate applies above that. For vehicles, you pay the sales tax when you register it at the DMV — and there's no getting around it by buying in a lower-tax county. The rate is based on where you live, not where you buy.

## Property Tax — The Big One

Property tax is where Florida actually gets you. Without an income tax, local governments rely heavily on property taxes to fund schools, fire rescue, libraries, and infrastructure. The rates vary significantly by county and even by municipality.

### How Florida Property Tax Works

Florida property tax is based on **millage rates** — mills per dollar of assessed value. One mill = $1 per $1,000 of taxable value. The total millage includes county, city, school district, and special district levies.

Your **taxable value** is your assessed value minus exemptions (like the [homestead exemption](/florida-homestead-exemption/), which knocks $50,000 off your taxable value).

**Important:** The assessed value for tax purposes isn't necessarily the market value. Florida has the "Save Our Homes" cap that limits increases on homesteaded properties to 3% per year or the CPI, whichever is less. So if you bought your home in 2020 and it's appreciated 40%, your assessed value may have only increased 15–18%. This creates a massive tax advantage for long-term homeowners.

### The Homestead Exemption — Don't Skip This

If you buy a home and make it your primary residence, you qualify for the [Florida Homestead Exemption](/florida-homestead-exemption/). This removes $50,000 from your taxable value — saving you roughly $875–$1,100/year depending on your county's millage rate. It also activates the Save Our Homes cap, which protects you from large tax increases going forward.

**File between January 1 and March 1** of the year after you purchase. Miss this deadline and you lose an entire year of savings. Read the full breakdown in our [Homestead Exemption guide](/florida-homestead-exemption/).

## Real Estate Transaction Taxes

When you buy or sell property in Florida, several taxes apply:

### Documentary Stamp Tax

Florida charges $0.70 per $100 of the sale price (called "doc stamps") on the deed. On a $400,000 home, that's $2,800. Typically paid by the seller in Tampa Bay, though this is negotiable.

There's also a doc stamp tax on the mortgage: $0.35 per $100 of the loan amount. On a $360,000 mortgage, that's $1,260. Paid by the buyer.

### Intangible Tax

A one-time tax on new mortgages: $0.002 per dollar (2 mills) of the mortgage amount. On a $360,000 mortgage, that's $720. Paid by the buyer at closing.

### Title Insurance

Not a tax, but a required cost. Florida title insurance rates are set by the state, so they don't vary by provider. On a $400,000 purchase, expect about $2,075 for the owner's policy.

## Business Taxes in Florida

### Corporate Income Tax

Florida does have a corporate income tax — 5.5% on income over $50,000 for C-corporations. However, S-corps, LLCs, and sole proprietorships pass through to your personal return, which means no state tax on business income for most small business owners.

### Tangible Personal Property Tax

If you own a business, you're taxed on your business equipment, furniture, and fixtures — anything that isn't real estate. You file a DR-405 form annually by April 1. There's a $25,000 exemption, so most home-based businesses and small operations owe nothing. But if you have significant equipment, this tax adds up.

### Commercial Rent Tax

Florida charges sales tax on commercial lease payments. The state rate dropped to 2% (from 5.5% over recent years) plus the county surtax. So in Hillsborough, you're paying 3.5% tax on your office lease. This is unusual — most states don't tax rent — and it's a real cost for businesses.

### Reemployment Tax (Unemployment)

If you have employees, you pay Florida's reemployment tax (their version of unemployment insurance) on the first $7,000 of each employee's wages. Rates vary by industry and your claims history, starting around 2.7% for new employers.

## Taxes That Catch People Off Guard

### Insurance Costs Replace Some Tax Savings

Florida's no-income-tax advantage is partially offset by higher insurance costs — particularly homeowners insurance and auto insurance. Budget $2,500–$5,000/year for homeowners insurance (more in flood zones or coastal areas) and auto insurance rates that rank among the highest in the nation due to uninsured motorists and litigation.

Compare rates before you move. {{policygenius}} lets you shop homeowners and auto insurance across multiple carriers to find the best rate for your situation.

### The "Snowbird Tax" Trap

If you're maintaining residences in both Florida and another state, be very careful about establishing your Florida domicile. States like New York and California aggressively audit former residents. You need to actually live in Florida for more than 183 days, change your driver's license, register to vote here, update your estate documents, and file a Declaration of Domicile with your county clerk. Half-measures can result in your former state claiming you owe income tax.

### Tangible Personal Property for Home-Based Businesses

If you run a business from home with more than $25,000 in equipment (computers, monitors, printers, furniture), you may owe tangible personal property tax. File the DR-405 form by April 1 even if you think you're under the threshold — failure to file can result in the Property Appraiser estimating your value, usually higher than reality.

### CDD Fees

Some newer communities (especially in [Wesley Chapel](/cities/wesley-chapel/), [Riverview](/cities/riverview/), and [Lakewood Ranch](/cities/lakewood-ranch/)) have Community Development Districts that levy their own assessments — sometimes $1,500–$3,000/year on top of property taxes and HOA fees. These aren't technically taxes, but they're non-negotiable and show up on your tax bill. Always check for CDDs before buying in a new-construction community.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Does Florida tax retirement income?
No. Pensions, 401(k) distributions, IRA withdrawals, Social Security, and annuity income are all tax-free at the state level. This is one of the biggest reasons retirees choose Florida.

### Is there a Florida estate or inheritance tax?
No. Florida has no estate tax and no inheritance tax. Florida even has constitutional protection against it — Article VII, Section 5(a) prohibits a state estate tax beyond the federal credit (which was repealed in 2005).

### Do I have to file a Florida state tax return?
No. There is no personal income tax return to file in Florida. If you own a C-corporation, you'll file a corporate return. Otherwise, you only deal with property taxes and sales tax.

### What if I work remotely for a company in a state with income tax?
You pay tax based on where you physically work, not where your company is located. If you live in Florida and work from home for a New York company, you owe no state income tax. However, some states (looking at you, New York) have "convenience of the employer" rules that can complicate this. Consult a tax professional for your specific situation.

### How does Florida fund its government without income tax?
Sales tax is the biggest revenue source, followed by corporate income tax, documentary stamp tax, tourism taxes, lottery revenue, and federal transfers. Property taxes fund local governments and schools. The model works because Florida's population growth and tourism generate massive sales tax revenue.

### Are property taxes higher in Florida to compensate for no income tax?
Compared to national averages, Florida's property tax rates are moderate — lower than New Jersey, Illinois, Connecticut, and New York, but higher than California and Hawaii. The effective rate on a homesteaded property in Tampa Bay is typically 0.8–1.1% of market value.
